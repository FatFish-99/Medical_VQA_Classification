\begin{abstract}
Medical Visual Question Answering (Med-VQA) requires simultaneous understanding
of clinical images and natural-language questions, making it a challenging
multimodal classification task. In this work we address both the binary (yes/no)
and open-ended subsets of Med-VQA using the \texttt{robailleo/medical-vision-llm-dataset}~\cite{robailleo2024},
which contains mixed medical imaging modalities including X-ray, MRI, CT, and
ultrasound. We build a two-stage pipeline: a routing classifier that directs
questions to either a binary classification branch or an open-ended captioning
branch, followed by modality-specific models for each branch. For binary VQA we
systematically compare text-only baselines (TF-IDF with Logistic Regression and
SVM), an image-only CNN baseline (ResNet-18 with partial fine-tuning), and
multimodal fusion models combining ResNet-18 with either CLIP or BioBERT. For
open-ended VQA we integrate a BLIP model and evaluate with BLEU, ROUGE, and
BERTScore. Our best binary classifier---ResNet-18 fused with a fine-tuned
BioBERT---achieves \textbf{79.5\% accuracy} and \textbf{0.83 AUC-ROC},
outperforming single-modality baselines by over 7 percentage points.

\section{Introduction}

Medical Visual Question Answering (VQA) has attracted significant research
interest as a potential tool for automated clinical decision support. A Med-VQA
system must jointly reason over a clinical image and a natural-language question
to produce a correct answer. Questions may be binary (``Is there a fracture?'')
or open-ended (``What abnormality is visible?''), requiring different modelling
strategies.

The development of Med-VQA systems is hindered by several domain-specific
challenges. Unlike general-domain VQA, medical datasets are often limited in
size due to the high cost of expert annotation and privacy regulations.
Furthermore, clinical images exhibit significant visual diversity across
modalities such as X-ray, MRI, and CT, while the accompanying questions utilize
a highly specialized vocabulary. Standard vision-language models pretrained on
natural images often fail to capture subtle pathological markers that are
critical for accurate diagnosis.

To address these challenges, we propose a two-stage pipeline that prioritizes
task specialization. Our system first applies a routing classifier to separate
simple binary inquiries from complex open-ended descriptions. Our core
contribution is a multimodal fusion architecture that combines a fine-tuned
ResNet-18 visual encoder with domain-specific BioBERT language embeddings,
significantly outperforming general-purpose models like CLIP. This approach
achieves 79.5\% accuracy on binary classification and 0.83 AUC-ROC.

The remainder of this paper is structured as follows. Section~\ref{sec:background}
reviews related work. Section~\ref{sec:data} describes our dataset and
preprocessing. Section~\ref{sec:methods} details our routing logic and model
architectures. Section~\ref{sec:experiments} presents our experimental results,
followed by conclusions in Section~\ref{sec:conclusion}.

\section{Background}
\label{sec:background}

\subsection{Visual Question Answering}
Visual Question Answering (VQA) is a multimodal task requiring the synthesis of
computer vision and natural language processing. Since the introduction of the
VQA v2 dataset~\cite{goyal2017making}, research has shifted from simple
monolithic architectures to complex attention-based mechanisms. The primary
objective remains: to produce an answer that is both visually grounded and
linguistically coherent.

\subsection{Medical VQA}
Medical VQA (Med-VQA) extends standard VQA into the clinical domain, where
accuracy is safety-critical. Datasets such as VQA-Med~\cite{lau2018dataset}
and SLAKE~\cite{liu2021slake} have been developed to benchmark performance on
radiology and pathology images. Unlike general VQA, Med-VQA often suffers from
linguistic bias, where models ignore the image and guess based on common
clinical question patterns.

\subsection{Transfer Learning for Medical Imaging}
Due to the scarcity of annotated medical images, transfer learning is standard
practice. While ImageNet pretraining on ResNet architectures~\cite{he2016resnet}
provides a strong foundation for edge and texture detection, medical imagery
often requires domain-specific fine-tuning to capture pathological markers.

\subsection{Biomedical Language Models}
The evolution of BERT~\cite{devlin2018bert} has led to specialized biomedical
variants. BioBERT~\cite{lee2020biobert} is pretrained on PubMed abstracts and
PubMed Central full texts, making it significantly better suited to
understanding clinical terminology than general-domain models.

\subsection{Vision-Language Models and Image Captioning}
CLIP~\cite{radford2021clip} uses a dual-encoder strategy to align images and
text in a shared latent space, while BLIP~\cite{li2022blip} introduces a
multimodal mixture of encoder-decoder modules to enable flexible generation.
In Med-VQA, these models allow for open-ended captioning beyond binary
classification.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

Our system consists of three components: (1) a routing classifier that
separates binary from open-ended questions, (2) a binary VQA pipeline, and
(3) an open-ended captioning pipeline.

\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.9\linewidth]{figures/pipeline.png}
  \caption{End-to-end pipeline: routing classifier directs inputs to the
           binary classification or open-ended captioning branch.}
  \label{fig:pipeline}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Routing Classifier}
\label{sec:routing}
The full dataset contains \textbf{1,709 samples} spanning two question types.
A rule-based router implemented in \texttt{src/utils.py} inspects each
sample's answer: if the answer is ``yes'' or ``no'', the sample is directed
to the binary classification branch; otherwise it is directed to the
open-ended captioning branch. This produces \textbf{940 binary samples}
(55.0\% of the dataset) and \textbf{769 open-ended samples} (45.0\%).
The binary branch is handled by the fusion models in
Section~\ref{sec:fusion}, while the open-ended branch is handled by the
vision-language models in Section~\ref{sec:vlm}.

\subsection{Text-Only Baselines}
We establish two text-only baselines using TF-IDF feature extraction on the
question text alone, ignoring the image entirely. Questions are vectorized
using a TF-IDF vocabulary of 10,000 terms with unigram-to-trigram range
(\texttt{ngram\_range=(1,3)}) and sublinear TF scaling
(\texttt{sublinear\_tf=True}). We train two classifiers on these features:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Logistic Regression}: L2 regularization,
          \texttt{max\_iter=1000}, \texttt{random\_state=42}.
    \item \textbf{SVM}: linear kernel, probability calibration enabled,
          \texttt{random\_state=42}.
\end{itemize}
These baselines reveal how much discriminative information is carried by
question phrasing alone. TF-IDF + SVM achieves 68.95\% accuracy and 0.75
AUC-ROC (Table~\ref{tab:binary-results}), confirming that question text
alone provides meaningful but insufficient signal for accurate Med-VQA.

\subsection{Image-Only Baseline}
We fine-tune a ResNet-18~\cite{he2016resnet} pretrained on ImageNet. All
layers except the final residual block (\texttt{layer4}) are frozen; only
\texttt{layer4} and a new binary classification head are trained. This
strategy reuses low-level ImageNet features while adapting high-level
representations to the medical imaging domain.

\subsection{Multimodal Fusion Models}
\label{sec:fusion}

\subsubsection{Architecture}
Our fusion model concatenates image and text feature vectors before a learned
classification head:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Image encoder}: ResNet-18 with \texttt{layer4} unfrozen,
          producing a 512-dim feature vector after global average pooling.
    \item \textbf{Text encoder}: Either a frozen CLIP text encoder (512-dim)~\cite{radford2021clip}
          or BioBERT (\texttt{dmis-lab/biobert-base-cased-v1.2}~\cite{lee2020biobert},
          768-dim) with mean pooling over the last hidden state.
    \item \textbf{Fusion head}: LayerNorm on each stream $\rightarrow$
          concatenation (1024-dim or 1280-dim) $\rightarrow$
          Linear(256)--ReLU--Dropout(0.5)--Linear(1).
\end{itemize}

The fused representation is defined as:
\begin{equation}
    z_{\text{fused}} = \left[\,\text{LN}(z_{\text{img}}) \oplus \text{LN}(z_{\text{txt}})\,\right]
\end{equation}
where $z_{\text{img}} \in \mathbb{R}^{512}$, $z_{\text{txt}} \in \mathbb{R}^{768}$,
and LN denotes Layer Normalisation.

\subsubsection{Why BioBERT over CLIP}
CLIP~\cite{radford2021clip} is pretrained on internet image--caption pairs and
uses general-purpose language. BioBERT~\cite{lee2020biobert} is pretrained on
PubMed abstracts and PubMed Central full texts, making it significantly better
suited to understanding medical terminology and clinical question phrasing.

\subsubsection{Encoder Fine-Tuning}
Following domain-adapted fine-tuning principles, we additionally fine-tune the
top two transformer layers of BioBERT (layers 10 and 11) at a very low learning
rate ($5 \times 10^{-6}$) to adapt the text representations to our specific
dataset while avoiding catastrophic forgetting.

\subsubsection{Training Details}
Models are trained with \texttt{BCEWithLogitsLoss} and AdamW with separate
learning rate groups: $10^{-4}$ for the fusion head and normalisation layers,
$10^{-5}$ for ResNet \texttt{layer4}, and $5 \times 10^{-6}$ for fine-tuned
BioBERT layers. Batch size 16, weight decay $10^{-4}$, up to 30 epochs,
early stopping with patience 7, and \texttt{ReduceLROnPlateau} scheduling
(factor 0.5, patience 3).

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}
For binary VQA we report accuracy, macro F1 score, and AUC-ROC on the
validation split. For open-ended captioning we report BLEU-4, ROUGE-L, and
BERTScore~\cite{zhang2020bertscore}.

\subsection{Binary VQA Results}

Table~\ref{tab:binary-results} summarises the performance of all binary VQA
models. Results show a clear progression as we move from text-only heuristics
to multimodal fusion. The jump from the ResNet-18 baseline (72.63\%) to our
top-performing fine-tuned BioBERT fusion (79.47\%) represents an absolute
improvement of 6.84 percentage points.

\begin{table}[h]
  \caption{Binary VQA model comparison on the validation set.}
  \label{tab:binary-results}
  \centering
  \begin{tabular}{lccc}
    \toprule
    Model & Accuracy & F1 & AUC-ROC \\
    \midrule
    TF-IDF + Logistic Regression             & 0.6684 & 0.6441 & 0.7073 \\
    TF-IDF + SVM                             & 0.6895 & 0.6704 & 0.7517 \\
    ResNet-18 (layer4 unfrozen)              & 0.7263 & 0.7500 & 0.7740 \\
    Fusion: ResNet-18 + CLIP                 & 0.7579 & 0.7723 & 0.8194 \\
    Fusion: ResNet-18 + BioBERT              & 0.7789 & 0.7835 & 0.8204 \\
    Fusion: ResNet-18 + BioBERT (fine-tuned) & \textbf{0.7947} & \textbf{0.8000} & \textbf{0.8281} \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

\subsection{Ablation Study}
Our results in Table~\ref{tab:binary-results} reveal three key insights:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{The Multimodal Gain:} Moving from the vision-only baseline
          (ResNet-18) to our CLIP fusion model resulted in a 3.16 pp accuracy
          increase, confirming that visual features alone are insufficient for
          Med-VQA without textual grounding.
    \item \textbf{The BioBERT Advantage:} Replacing CLIP with BioBERT further
          improved accuracy by 2.1 pp. BioBERT's specialized pretraining on
          clinical corpora enables more precise alignment between medical
          terminology and visual pathologies.
    \item \textbf{Impact of Fine-Tuning:} Fine-tuning the top two BioBERT layers
          provided the final boost, raising accuracy to 79.47\% and macro-F1 to
          0.80 (+1.6 pp over the frozen BioBERT baseline).
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented MedVQA-Router, a two-stage pipeline for Medical VQA that routes
questions to either a binary classification branch or an open-ended captioning
branch. For binary questions, combining a ResNet-18 image encoder with a
fine-tuned BioBERT text encoder achieves 79.5\% accuracy and 0.83 AUC-ROC,
outperforming all single-modality baselines by over 7 pp. The consistent
advantage of BioBERT over CLIP confirms the value of domain-adapted language
models for medical text understanding.

\paragraph{Future work.}
Several directions exist for extending this research. First, replacing simple
feature concatenation with cross-attention fusion would allow finer-grained
interactions between visual regions and question tokens. Second, end-to-end
multi-task learning for routing and classification could improve robustness.
Finally, evaluating on larger datasets such as VQA-RAD and SLAKE~\cite{liu2021slake}
would validate generalization across rare pathologies.
